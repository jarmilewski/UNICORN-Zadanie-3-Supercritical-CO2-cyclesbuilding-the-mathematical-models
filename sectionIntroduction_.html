Renewable power sources, e.g. solar power \cite{Siddiqui_2017}, are highly variable in terms of output in many parts of the world, hence their development should go be followed by methods for their integration with energy systems e.g.  integration of solar power with CCS sytem  \cite{Bonaventura_2018} . Another very promising method for dealing with variability of renewable resources  is development of energy storage systems such as e.g. liquid/compressed air energy storage \cite{Krawczyk_2018,ISI:000415769300002}, thermal energy storage  \cite{Le_ko_2017}.  One of the most prospective energy storage concepts seems to be power-to-gas-to-power  \cite{Kotowicz_2017}. The power-to-gas-to-power  system consist of gas turbine co-firing hydrogen which is supplied from a distributed electrolysis installations  \cite{Garc_a_Cl_a_2018,Nadar_2018,Zarei_Senseni_2017,Fukuzumi_2017,Chen_2017,Zhang_2017,Olivier_2017} , powered by the wind farms located a short distance from the potential construction site of the gas turbine  \cite{ISI:000431450000009,Zhuang_2017} .  The high temperature fuel cells are being considered as one of the next generation electric power sources because of its eco-friendliness and high efficiency advantages (with contrary to CHP systems based on internal combustion engines  \cite{Carlucci_2017} , hence fuel cells such as SOFC, \cite{Kupecki_2018,Prokop_2018,Genc_2018,Azizi_2018,De_Lorenzo_2018,Ferrel_lvarez_2018,ISI:000417079400026,Peksen_2018,ISI:000433378300009,Dillig_2018,2017,Kupecki_2017,Zheng_2017,Barelli_2017}, H+SOFC \cite{Dzierzgowski_2018,Danilov_2017,ISI:000413004200059} and MCFC \cite{Davoodi_2018,ISI:000430563900009,Jienkulsawad_2018,Wu_2017,Accardo_2017,Cheon_2013,Samanta_2017,Czelej_2017} should be considered as a method for energy recovery from hydrogen.  Furthermore, since MCFC operates at temperature range of 600–700 °C, which matches well with the operating temperature range of a S–CO2 Brayton cycle, the S–CO2 Brayton could be applied  as a bottoming cycle to improve the total system performance  \cite{Bae_2014}.
 
In the 1960s, Feher \citep{Feher196885} studied the properties of various gases with a view to determining the most suitable one for a supercritical thermodynamic cycle. Carbon dioxide was proposed as a working fluid for several reasons. First, its physical properties e.g. critical pressure, which is significantly lower than water, allows lower operating pressures. Second, the thermodynamic and transport properties of CO2 are well known, hence cycle analysis is based on reasonably firm data. Finally, carbon dioxide is abundant, non-toxic and is relatively low cost. The analysis proved that the CO2 supercritical cycle offers several desirable features: high thermal efficiency (the investigated cycle achieved thermal efficiency of 55% under ideal conditions), low volume-to-power ratio and no blade corrosion or cavitation. The paper suggests using it for electric power generation (both terrestrial and space) or to provide shaft power for marine propulsion. Research on the supercritical CO2 power cycles was resumed by Dostal four decades later. In 2004 \citep{Dostal2004} performed a systematic, detailed major component and system design evaluation and multi-parameter optimization of the family of supercritical CO2 Brayton power cycles for application to advanced nuclear reactors. Later, Driscroll \citep{Driscroll2004} presented a report on cost projections for the supercritical CO2 Brayton indirect power cycle as applied to GEN-IV advanced reactors. These two papers have spawned a number of further studies of the CO2 supercritical cycle in the field of parameters and layout optimization, possible applications and modeling of critical cycle components.
