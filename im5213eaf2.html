Due to relatively small sizes of the super critical cycles, the turbomachinery used is also relatively small and often based on radial constructions (Figs \ref{div-198816} and \ref{div-327709} ). The modeling of expanders and compressor mainly based on energy balance equations with assumption of constant efficiency, see \cite{dostal2004supercritical}. More advanced studies like \cite{Pickard2010}, take into consideration models which use the mean--line flow analysis performance prediction tool for off-normal performance maps. This required multi--dimension look up tables (4D tables) for inlet temperature, pressure, mass flow rate, and shaft speed. Then the values are found by interpolating between these inlet parameters to determine the outlet temperature and pressure. This approach worked reasonably well, provided the data stayed within the stated range, but it was very labor intensive to generate the 4D maps. In fact, we used here the maps taken from this source.
For designing purposes a detailed CFD can be used for the modeling of the CO2 flow between turbomachinery blades, as shown in \cite{Pecnik2011,Schmitt_2014,Liu_2018}; or simple way by using the similarity concept \cite{zhao2017selection}. Also, commercially available software is used for estimating the main dimensions of the turbomachinery \cite{Nassar_2014}. A novel approach to calculate the performance map of a centrifugal compressor stage is presented in \cite{Casey_2012}. At the design point four nondimensional parameters were used to characterize the performance. In the new method the performance of the whole map was also based on these four parameters through physically based algebraic equations which require little prior knowledge of the detailed geometry.
A 1-dimensional tool for preliminary design and performance prediction of CO2
compressor is presented in \cite{Kus_2013}. The model describes high speed centrifugal compressor in a hermetic configuration supported on foil gas bearings. The model was validated against the total compressor efficiency of a 50 kW compressor published Sandia Laboratories.
Heat exchangers in supercritical CO2 cycles operate at relatively low temperatures and high pressures. Paper \cite{Serrano_2014} is focused on the methodology employed for the design and sizing of all the heat exchangers of the supercritical CO2 Brayton cycle. Due to the large pressure difference between the fluids, and also to their compactness, Printed Circuit Heat Exchangers (PCHE) are suggested for these type of cycles. Different empirical correlations for the pressure drop and the Nusselt number have been coupled and assessed. The design of the precooler and the low temperature recuperator are also verified by simulations using CFD due to the fact that they operate near-critical point of CO2. Experimental and numerical investigations on forced convection heat transfer of supercritical CO2 within
a semi-circular printed circuit heat exchanger have been performed in the present study. Primary operational
parameters included inlet pressure of 7.5 MPa to 8.5 MPa, mass fluxes ranged from 160 kg/m2s to
760 kg/m2s and the average heat fluxes of 20 kW/m2 to 90 kW/m2. A physically improved semi-empirical
correlation for the forced convection heat transfer of supercritical CO2 within the PCHE was developed by
implementing probability density function (PDF)-based property revised technique for more reasonably
interpreting the influence of the instantaneous turbulent temperature and fluctuating properties. The
comparison results showed that the simple DB correlation could not reproduce the experiments and
the Jackson correlation with the properties ratio correction terms could qualitatively capture the heat
transfer features under different working conditions, however, it still overestimated the average value
of the heat transfer coefficients in the whole range of the working conditions. The predictions of the
PDF-based new developed correlation are significantly improved so that more than 96% data in the heating
mode and more than 92% data in the cooling mode with various heat fluxes are captured and collapsed
to an ideal straight line within an accuracy of 25%.@
