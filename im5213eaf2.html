Due to relatively small sizes of the super critical cycles, the turbomachinery used is also relatively small and often based on radial constructions (Figs \ref{div-198816} and \ref{div-327709} ). The modeling of expanders and compressor mainly based on energy balance equations with assumption of constant efficiency, see \cite{dostal2004supercritical}. More advanced studies like \cite{Pickard2010}, take into consideration models which use the mean--line flow analysis performance prediction tool for off-normal performance maps. This required multi--dimension look up tables (4D tables) for inlet temperature, pressure, mass flow rate, and shaft speed. Then the values are found by interpolating between these inlet parameters to determine the outlet temperature and pressure. This approach worked reasonably well, provided the data stayed within the stated range, but it was very labor intensive to generate the 4D maps. In fact, we used here the maps taken from this source.
For designing purposes a detailed CFD can be used for the modeling of the CO2 flow between turbomachinery blades, as shown in \cite{Pecnik2011,Schmitt_2014}; or simple way by using the similarity concept \cite{zhao2017selection}. Also, commercially available software is used for estimating the main dimensions of the turbomachinery \cite{Nassar_2014}. A novel approach to calculate the performance map of a centrifugal compressor stage is
presented in \cite{Casey_2012}. At the design point four nondimensional parameters were used 
characterize
the performance. In the new method the performance of the whole map is also based on
these four parameters through physically based algebraic equations which require little
prior knowledge of the detailed geometry. The variable empirical coefficients in the parameterized
equations can be calibrated to match the performance maps of a wide range of
stage types, including turbocharger and process compressor impellers with vaned and
vaneless diffusers. The examples provided show that the efficiency and the pressure ratio
performance maps of turbochargers with vaneless diffusers can be predicted to within
62% in this way. More uncertainty is present in the prediction of the surge line, as this is
very variable from stage to stage. During the preliminary design the method provides a
useful reference performance map based on earlier experience for comparison with
